Yugen and Nonduality in Ghost in the Shell (1995)

by Arden Wang

A cyborg programmed to lack and disregard sentience, Major Motoko Kusanagi muses on what kind of answers about her identity she is yet to discover. Amid a futuristic, post-war backdrop, the Japanese anime classic Ghost in the Shell (1995) critiques the concept of identity through the protagonist’s inner trife and highlights the nuanced nondualism between man and machine, and the uncertainty and confusion echoed throughout the story evokes a dominantly melancholic mood throughout the film that beautifully captures the essence of Japanese aesthetics.

Ghost in the Shell does a profound job of communicating yugen through scenes without dialogue and just instrumentals, such as when Motoko is looking down from a skyscraper at the city, looking into the glossy windows and observing the people from afar. Yugen describes the feeling of coming upon a scene that elicits a profound sense of awe because of its natural and artistic beauty. What is special about yugen is its non-dualistic aspect of perceptual and interior depth: the scene that presents itself has physical depth to it, and the emotions that result involve reflection within the individual. There is a darkness and unknown to yugen, and embraces the obscure and mysterious quality of the universe.

Motoko often dives in the expansive seawater in the outskirts of the city, an activity that is described as “strange” for a cyborg. When she submerges her body underwater, we see that the environment is serene, devoid of life, and nothing but a vibrant blue that transitions into a murky black. Yugen is present in the physical depth of the water, exhibited by the pitch black darkness into which she sinks into. This profound combination of physical and emotional depth is a quintessential example of yugen. She rises to the surface, and is mirrored by her reflection. The emotional depth emerges as her contemplations. In the scene, she says that being in the sea brings her “fear, anxiety, isolation, and darkness” and sometimes hope. When she floats to the water, she feels like she’s becoming someone else.

A computer program that somehow gained sentience over the years, Project 2501 wishes to be more human, but knows that it is limited in becoming human because of its inability to reproduce. Motoko and Project 2501 (the Puppet Master) are by far the most complex characters that arise from Ghost in the Shell, and are key examples of non-duality in the climax of the film. When Project 2501 and Motoko are lying side by side and telepathically communicating, Project 2501 declares that it and Motoko are one and the same, for they share the same qualities of being non-human but self-aware. As separate but interwoven entities, their merging – a surprising moment in the film – is a physical manifestation of nonduality. Project 2501 is also a key example of reflexivity: despite achieving self-awareness, it knows that it is but a self-aware program that has limitations from the complete human experience (reproduction and death). When Motoko “dies”, she catches a glimpse of an angel, which indicates her transcendence.
